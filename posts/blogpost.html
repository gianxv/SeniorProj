Finding Troubles in Filipino Identity

This feeling of disconnect is deeply rooted in complex historical, social, and personal factors that have shaped the experiences of us Filipinos.

Colonial Mentality

Being Filipino has always been more complicated than it looks on paper. The disconnect many of us feel—from our culture, our language, even from each other—isn’t just a personal confusion. It’s a wound shaped by colonization, migration, and generations of trying to fit into places that never fully made space for us. Growing up, I struggled with speaking Tagalog fluently. My parents told me to speak English so I could “succeed.” But now, I feel the weight of what was lost—the songs I can’t sing along to, the jokes I only half-understand, the stories my lola tells that get lost in translation. There’s also the tension of being “not Filipino enough” to some and “too Filipino” to others. In school, I was teased for bringing baon that smelled different. At home, I was reminded I was forgetting where I came from. This trouble with identity isn’t about not being proud. It’s about navigating the broken bridges left behind by history, by colonizers, by distance. But maybe, naming these troubles is the first step toward healing. Maybe in our confusion, we’ll find a deeper understanding of who we are—and who we can become.
